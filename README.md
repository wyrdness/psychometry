## Overview

Psychometry (also called token-object reading or object reading) is a claimed psi modality in which a sensitive holds or touches an object and reports impressions of its history, owners, or associated events. American physician and Eclectic Medical Institute professor Joseph Rodes Buchanan coined the term in 1842, theorising that all things give off persistent emanations that could be 'measured' by trained sensitives; he developed the concept further in his Manual of Psychometry (1893). Geologist William Denton extended the practice into 'geological psychometry' with The Soul of Things (1863), in which his sister and wife reported impressions from rock and fossil specimens. Through the late 19th and early 20th centuries psychometry was a popular feature of Spiritualist seances and stage performances. Selected mediums — notably Stefan Ossowiecki in interwar Poland and Gerard Croiset in the mid-20th-century Netherlands — were tested by parapsychologists and remain reference cases in the field. Modern parapsychology absorbs psychometry-like tasks into broader ESP / clairvoyance protocols. The mainstream scientific consensus is that there is no replicable evidence for psychometry; apparent successes are well explained by cold reading, confirmation bias, and selective reporting. Police agencies overwhelmingly do not consider psychic tipsters useful.

- **Origin**: Coined in 1842 by Joseph Rodes Buchanan from Greek psychē ('soul, mind') and metron ('measure')
- **Meaning**: Literally 'soul-measuring'; the measurement of an object's psychic 'emanations'
- **First use**: Buchanan, J. R. (c.1842); developed in his Manual of Psychometry (1893)

### Buchanan's early letter-reading experiments ⭐
> **Date**: 1842-01-01  
> **Location**: Cincinnati / Eclectic Medical Institute environs  
Buchanan reported experiments in which subjects holding sealed letters described the writers' personalities and circumstances; he interpreted these as evidence of measurable psychic emanations from physical objects.
**Credibility**: low — Foundational claim of psychometry; would not satisfy modern methodological standards.
**Sources**: Buchanan, J. R., Manual of Psychometry (1893)

### Denton 'Soul of Things' geological psychometry ⭐
> **Date**: 1863-01-01  
> **Location**: United States; experiments in Boston-area scientific circles  
Geologist William Denton presented his sister and wife with rock and fossil specimens whose origins he had not disclosed; their reported impressions of geological epochs and origins were collected in The Soul of Things (1863).
**Credibility**: low — Influential Spiritualist case; not regarded as evidence by modern standards.
**Sources**: Denton, W., & Denton, E. M. F. (1863), The Soul of Things

### Tenhaeff testing of Gerard Croiset (Utrecht) ⭐
> **Date**: 1953-01-01  
> **Location**: University of Utrecht parapsychology laboratory  
Dutch parapsychologist W. H. C. Tenhaeff conducted decades of object-reading and 'chair test' experiments with the medium Gerard Croiset. After Croiset's death in 1980, Piet Hein Hoebens's re-analysis showed extensive selective reporting and methodological flaws.
**Credibility**: low — Often cited in psi literature; widely regarded as a cautionary tale.
**Sources**: Hoebens, P. H. (1981), 'Croiset and Professor Tenhaeff: Discrepancies in Claims of Clairvoyance', Skeptical Inquirer

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Persistent emanations from objects** _(Joseph Rodes Buchanan, William Denton, various Spiritualist mediums)_

Buchanan's original theory: every object retains psychic 'emanations' of its history that a trained sensitive can detect on contact.

**Object-mediated clairvoyance / retrocognition** _(W. H. C. Tenhaeff, Some Parapsychological Association members)_

Modern parapsychological framing in which the object serves as a focusing 'target' for general clairvoyant or retrocognitive ability rather than as a literal repository of emanations.

### Skeptical

**Cold reading and confirmation bias** _(Ray Hyman, Joe Nickell, Susan Blackmore)_

Apparent psychometric successes are produced by cold reading (broadly applicable statements, fishing, and observation of cues), confirmation bias, and selective recall of hits.

**Selective reporting and uncontrolled provenance** _(Piet Hein Hoebens, James Randi)_

Famous psychometry cases (Croiset / Tenhaeff, various Spiritualist mediums) involved selective reporting and inadequate blinding of object provenance.

**No replicable laboratory evidence** _(Ray Hyman, Chris French, Richard Wiseman)_

When tested under modern double-blind protocols, psychometry-like tasks produce results indistinguishable from chance.
